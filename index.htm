You Are Not Alone in This.

Struggling does not mean you have failed.

Many young Asian Americans carry invisible weight: the pressure to achieve, to hold the family together, to never show weakness. If something feels heavy inside, that feeling deserves care. And you deserve support.

You are not unusual

This is far more common than you think.

Depression and anxiety among young Asian Americans occur at similar rates to the general population. The difference is not how many people struggle. It is how many feel they can ask for help.

Because of cultural pressures to appear well, most people around you are also quietly managing more than they show. The person next to you in class, your coworker, your friend: statistically, many of them carry the same weight.

The silence around mental health in Asian communities does not mean the pain is not there. It means it goes unseen and untreated. That silence has a cost, and you do not have to be part of it.

Intergenerational conflict, arising when US-born children adopt more open attitudes toward mental health that clash with family values, creates high-stress environments that deter young people from seeking care.

Understanding the context

How Confucian values shape our relationship with asking for help.

Confucian philosophy has shaped family and social life across China, Korea, Japan, Vietnam, and their diaspora communities for over two thousand years. These values are a genuine source of strength, community, and identity. But when applied to mental health, they can create invisible barriers that are hard to name and harder to overcome.

Understanding where these barriers come from is the first step to moving through them, without abandoning the values that matter to you.

Filial Piety
孝 / 효 / 孝行 / Hiếu thảo

Deep respect and loyalty to parents and ancestors is a core virtue. Family honour is collective: individual actions reflect on everyone.

This can lead to hiding struggles to avoid shaming the family, or feeling that seeking outside help is a betrayal of loyalty.

Self-Restraint
克己 / ,ทก&#AE30; / 克己 / Tự kiềm chế

Emotional discipline and inner control are signs of virtue. Expressing distress openly can be seen as a lack of self-mastery.

Struggling emotionally becomes framed as a personal failure rather than a human and medical reality.

Face (面子)
面子 / 체면 / 面目 / Thể diện

Social standing and reputation, for both individual and family, are deeply important. Maintaining face shapes many decisions.

Mental health treatment is avoided because it feels like admitting something that could damage the family’s image in the community.

Harmony
和 / 화 / 和 / Hòa thuận

Social and family harmony is valued above individual expression. Disrupting the peace, even to address real suffering, can feel wrong.

Young people suppress symptoms to avoid disrupting family dynamics, especially if parents might be dismissive of mental health concerns.

These values are not the enemy. Filial piety, restraint, and harmony are genuine virtues. The goal is not to discard them, but to expand what they allow. Caring for your mental health is an act of filial piety: it lets you be more present, more stable, and more capable of showing up for the people you love.

What you might be carrying

These feelings are real, and they have names.

If any of these sound familiar, you are not imagining it. And you are not alone.

Exhaustion

Tired in a way sleep cannot fix. Worn out by performing wellness while feeling anything but.

Loneliness

Surrounded by people, but unable to show what is really happening inside. No safe place to be honest.

Pressure

The weight of family expectations, academic demands, and the model minority standard.

Between Worlds

Caught between cultures, not fully belonging to either. Unsure which version of yourself is real.

Numbness

Feeling disconnected, going through the motions. Like something is switched off inside.

Shame

The belief that feeling this way means something is fundamentally wrong with you. It does not. It never did.

Shifting the lens

Thoughts that keep us silent, and another way to hold them.

The familiar thought
Another way to see it

“I should handle this on my own. Asking for help is weakness.”

Seeking support is discipline: the discipline to care for yourself so you can carry your responsibilities fully. The strongest people recognise their limits.

“If my family finds out I am in therapy, they will be ashamed.”

Mental health care is confidential. You have the right to make decisions about your own health privately. Many people seek help without anyone in their family knowing.

“Other people have it worse. I do not deserve to complain.”

Pain is not a competition. Your experience is real and valid on its own terms. Others suffering more does not make yours less deserving of care.

“This is just stress. Everyone feels like this. It will pass.”

Some stress is normal. But persistent exhaustion or anxiety that disrupts your life is a signal worth listening to, not pushing through alone.

For parents and guardians

Your child is not broken. And neither are you.

If your child has told you they are struggling, or if you have sensed something is wrong, it can feel frightening and confusing. This is especially true if mental health was never discussed openly in your own upbringing. This section is for you.

“You raised your children to be strong, to work hard, to make the family proud. When they come to you and say they are struggling, it can feel like a failure, yours or theirs. But it is neither. It means they trust you enough to tell you. That trust is the most important thing. Please do not turn it away.”

A message to every parent who wants to understand

Common beliefs and what the evidence shows

Stereotypes and stigmas that get in the way

Common Belief

“Mental illness is the result of weak character or lack of self-discipline.”

What we know

Mental health conditions are medical realities with biological, environmental, and social causes, the same as diabetes or a broken bone. Character has nothing to do with it.

Common Belief

“Asian families do not have these problems. This is a Western concept.”

What we know

Depression and anxiety are global conditions. Young people across East and Southeast Asia experience mental health conditions at similar rates. They are simply far less likely to receive treatment.

Common Belief

“If they eat well, exercise, and focus on their studies, they will be fine.”

What we know

Healthy habits support wellbeing but cannot replace treatment for clinical depression or anxiety. Telling someone to try harder when they are struggling medically can deepen their distress.

Common Belief

“Seeing a therapist means the whole community will know. It brings shame to the family.”

What we know

Mental health care is legally confidential. Therapists cannot share your child’s information without consent. Seeking help is a private decision, and one that often prevents far greater crises later.

If your child comes to you
  • →Listen without immediately trying to fix. Being heard is often the most healing thing.
  • →Avoid phrases like “you have nothing to be sad about” or “think of how hard I worked for you.” These shut down conversation.
  • →Ask: “What do you need from me right now?” Let them guide you.
  • →Supporting them in accessing professional care is one of the most loving things you can do as a parent.
